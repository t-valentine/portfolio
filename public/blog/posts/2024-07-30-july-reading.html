#16: The Reaping by Bernard Taylor

I started this blog at the end of May and only started writing book entires in June. As a result, I've never written on any of the 'Paperbacks from Hell.' Paperbacks from Hell is an illustration heavy book by Grady Hendrix about the history of paperback horror. There's a strong emphasis on the cover art done for the novels. After that book was published, 21 old horror books were re-published with their original painted covers. I read a couple last year because I like old cheesy horror and read The Nest in January. The Reaping is another one of the republished books.

The book is about a widower who owns an art supplies shop and is encouraged by a girlfriend to enter his paintings into a local art show. After the show, he's approached by a woman looking to have a portrait made of her employer's niece. He goes to the employer's estate for a week or two to paint the portrait and creepy things ensue.

This is a "slow" book in terms of revealing itself but the character writing and tension is written so well. Because I'm kind of familiar with witchcraft-related superstitions, I was able to figure out the twist about halfway through the book. This did not impact by enjoyment of the book. I really enjoyed it and will be seeking out some of the other things Bernard Taylor wrote.

#17: Under the Pendulum Sun by Jeannette Ng

The copy I got from the library had the "SciFi" sticker on the spine, however this novel is a capital G fantasy Gothic novel. I found this book from a tumblr post that urged enjoyers of gothic fiction to check out this book.

It is impossible to look up anything about this book without finding (what I believe to be) a major spoiler of the work. It's an adult gothic work and I think if you are comfortable with similar works, then you won't be bothered. The book is not terribly graphic in the physicality of the spoiler and I believe it's used very effectively in the book.

The story takes place in an alternate past, where the British empire found the land of the Fae and began trade with the fae. As with all things relating to empires, they also begun sending Christian missionaries. The story follows Catherine, a woman who's missionary brother has not written her in several months. She journeys to Arcadia (land of the Fae) to find him. She succeeds in the first third of the book, and the remainder of the book is about a Fairy Queen's visit and Catherine's determination to discover truth's about the (creepy) estate she and her brother now live in.

I don't believe the book completely comes together, and I think there are some checks the author doesn't cash. However, the main conflict is very convincingly written and the world building is really interesting. I would wholeheartedly recommend this book. It's also the author's first novel and a really great first novel. I'll be interested to see what Jeannette Ng publishes next.

P.S. I was raised Christian, so I'm familiar with the biblical references in the book. I have no idea how this book would read to someone without background in Christian theology and stories. The knowledge isn't mandatory to understand and enjoy the book, but I think it may change how you engage with it.

#18: Between Two Fires by Christopher Buehlman

This book did make me cry at the end and I ordered a copy for my home library before getting halfway through the book. It's a terrific book and hits one of my soft points, which is why I cried.

The premise of the book is pretty simple: what if the Black Plague was actually the forces of Hell trying to goad God into another holy war? And what if, not content with the suffering the plague alone created, demons also began roaming the earth.

It seems like I would have a similiar note for this that I wrote for Under the Pendulum Sun and Christianity, but I don't. While this book is really more about religion (seeing as it's literal demons causing the problems), I think it's the parts of Christianity that are very mainstream in American culture. It's much more well thought out than something like Supernatural, but I think it's at a similar level of necessary knowledge to engage with the piece.

The book follows a knight, a priest, and a young girl as they travel through Europe. The girl (Delphine) is convinced that they need to travel a certain path and it's a bit of a road trip book in that regard. The book is slightly episodic, but I didn't find it grating or repetitive. Like a good monster of the week show, each "episode" reveals something new about the characters and the nature of their world. I briefly mentioned it up top, but this is a horror novel and is sufficiently creepy and graphically violent. It's a really terrific description of hell and demons.

This book also does have a gay character that's well-written and appropriate for the time frame, but also written very lovingly. There's homophobia, but it doesn't feel mean.

Once again, I cried at the end. I don't think I can even do a good job describing this book. It's maybe the best book I've read this year.
